## Overview

Patupaiarehe (also called tūrehu) are fairy-like beings in Māori mythology—pale-skinned, light-haired supernatural people who dwell in mist-shrouded forests and mountaintops. **These mysterious beings predate human settlement** and represent the otherworldly inhabitants of Aotearoa's wilderness.

Unlike most Polynesian peoples who are brown-skinned, patupaiarehe are described as having **white or very pale skin and red or fair hair**. They avoid sunlight, emerging at dawn, dusk, and during foggy weather. Patupaiarehe live in communities, play haunting music on flutes, and sometimes interact with humans—occasionally taking human partners or children.

**CULTURAL NOTE**: Patupaiarehe are part of **living Māori tradition**, representing the relationship between human and spiritual realms, and the mysterious forces of the forest.

---

## Physical Description

Traditional descriptions of patupaiarehe:

- **Skin**: Pale white or very fair
- **Hair**: Red, blonde, or light-colored (unusual for Polynesians)
- **Eyes**: Sometimes light-colored
- **Build**: Similar to humans but often described as slightly smaller and more delicate
- **Clothing**: Cloaks of mist, traditional garments of fine weaving
- **Appearance**: Beautiful and otherworldly

**Distinguishing Features**
- Extreme paleness contrasting with Māori appearance
- Aversion to light and heat
- Association with mist and fog
- Ethereal, ghost-like quality
