## Overview

Patupaiarehe are fairy-like supernatural beings in the traditions of Maori iwi (tribes) across both islands of Aotearoa / New Zealand. They are described as pale to fair-skinned, with blonde or red hair, light-coloured eyes, and untattooed faces, and as living in invisible communities deep in forests and on mist-shrouded mountain tops. Patupaiarehe are nocturnal or active in mist and fog and are said to be harmed or driven off by direct sunlight, fire, light, cooked food, and strong smells. In some accounts they are roughly human-sized, while certain Whanganui traditions describe them as standing more than two metres tall and Tuhoe traditions describe them as small. They are skilled musicians of the koauau and putorino flutes and use this music to lure people into the forest. Maori traditions describe specific encounters - for example, the explorer Ihenga at Mount Ngongotaha, the patupaiarehe woman Kaiheraki and the hunter Hautapu, and Hinerehia who is said to have taught Maori women the art of weaving. The tradition is treated by Maori communities as part of a living spiritual landscape, distinct from the European fairy tradition with which it has been compared.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Maori. The names patupaiarehe and turehu are inherited Maori terms; pakepakeha is a related variant sometimes proposed as one source for the modern Maori word pakeha (used for New Zealanders of European descent).
- **Meaning**: There is no agreed gloss for patupaiarehe in standard reference works; the term denotes a class of supernatural forest people (he iwi atua).
- **First use**: Recorded in 19th-century Maori manuscript traditions and ethnographies, including Sir George Grey's collections (1840s-1850s) and the work of James Cowan in the early 20th century.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**He iwi atua - a people of the spirit world** _(Maori oral tradition)_

In Maori tradition, patupaiarehe are real supernatural beings, members of the spiritual landscape of Aotearoa, with their own communities and territories.

### Scientific

**Cultural-memory hypothesis (earlier inhabitants)** _(Some early 20th-century Pakeha ethnographers)_

A long-running but contested hypothesis suggested patupaiarehe traditions preserve a memory of earlier non-Polynesian inhabitants of New Zealand. This 'pre-Maori' theory is rejected by mainstream archaeology and Maori scholarship.

### Skeptical

**Folkloric and cosmological figures** _(Te Ara Encyclopedia of New Zealand contributors, Maori cultural authorities)_

Folklorists and Maori scholars treat patupaiarehe as authentic Maori spiritual figures within a coherent cosmology, rather than as evidence of literal hidden peoples.
